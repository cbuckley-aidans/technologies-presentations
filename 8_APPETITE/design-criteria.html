Key Concept
What is a design criterion?
Definition
A design criterion is a rule or standard your design must meet. Together, your criteria form a checklist you can test against when your design is finished.
Think of it like a referee's rulebook. Without clear rules, how do you know who wins? Without clear criteria, how do you know if your design succeeded?
The Goal
Write 4–6 criteria for your project. They should cover different aspects: how it works, how it looks, how easy it is to use, its physical properties, or its environmental impact.
Example: Lunch Box Project
✓   Must not leak when shaken upside-down for 10 seconds
✓   Must weigh less than 500g when empty
✓   Must keep food at least 20°C above room temperature for 4 hours
✓   Must survive a 75cm drop test without cracking
What makes a criterion bad?
❌ Too Vague
"Must be light."
Light compared to what? For who? You can't measure "light"; two people will disagree on whether you've met it.
Fix it: Use a specific number. "Must weigh less than 400g."
❌ Untestable
"Must look cool."
"Cool" is an opinion. There's no test you can run to prove whether your design is objectively cool.
Fix it: Use a survey. "Must be rated appealing by 8/10 students in our class."
❌ Unrealistic
"Must be made from titanium."
If you can't actually achieve it with your time, budget, and materials, it's not a useful criterion.
Fix it: Use available materials. "Must be made from recycled cardboard or plastic."
A fourth trap: Irrelevant. "My beach toy must be fireproof." True, but completely beside the point. Every criterion should solve a real problem for your users.
I Do: Framework
The 4-question test
Before writing each criterion, ask yourself these four questions.
1
Is it specific?
Does it use numbers, quantities, or named standards instead of vague words like "good", "light", "strong"?
2
Can you test it?
Is there a clear method to check if you've met it? A drop test, a timer, a scale, a survey?
3
Is it achievable?
Can you actually reach it with the materials, time, and skills you have available in this class?
4
Is it relevant?
Does it solve a real problem for your actual users? If no one cares about it, cut it.
Worked Example
Vague → Good
I Do
Project: Design a school water bottle for Year 8 students.
❌ Original (Vague) Criterion
"The water bottle must be easy to carry and not too heavy."
1
What does "easy to carry" mean? There's no measurable standard.
2
"Not too heavy": too heavy for who? A 12-year-old? An adult?
3
There's no test we can run to verify this criterion. It fails question 1 and 2.
✓ Improved Criteria (same idea, now specific)
1.   The bottle must weigh less than 450g when empty.
2.   It must fit in a standard school bag side pocket (≤ 8cm diameter).
3.   It must be possible to open the lid with one hand in under 3 seconds.
One vague criterion  →  three specific, testable ones
Notice: all three use numbers or physical tests. A scale, a ruler, and a stopwatch can verify each one. No argument needed.
Spot the Flaw
We Do
For each criterion below, jot down on your whiteboard: what is wrong with it? Use the 4 questions. Hold up when ready.
Criterion A: Backpack
"The backpack must be made from a good quality material that lasts a long time."
Criterion B: App
"The app must have an AI built in so it is really impressive."
Criterion C: Chair
"The chair must be made from carbon fibre and cost less than $5 to produce."
Criterion D: Planner
"The homework planner must include an underwater-proof seal on all pages."
Is it: too vague? Untestable? Unrealistic? Irrelevant? More than one?
Debrief
Spot the Flaw: Answers
A
"Made from good quality material that lasts a long time."
Vague + Untestable. "Good quality" and "long time" have no measurement. Fix: "Must withstand 500 cycles of opening/closing without tearing at the zipper."
B
"Must have an AI built in so it is really impressive."
Vague + Unrealistic + Untestable. "Impressive" can't be tested. AI integration may be beyond scope. Fix: Focus on what the app must actually do for users.
C
"Made from carbon fibre and cost less than $5."
Unrealistic (contradictory). Carbon fibre costs hundreds of dollars per metre, making it physically impossible to meet both requirements simultaneously.
D
"Homework planner must include an underwater-proof seal."
Irrelevant. Students don't use planners underwater. This doesn't address any real user need. Drop it and replace with something meaningful.
Sort It: Good or Bad?
We Do
Turn and talk: which of these criteria would you accept, and which need fixing? Be ready to explain why.
Criterion 1: Water filter
"The filter must remove at least 99% of visible particles in a standard tap-water sample, verified by comparing before/after photos."
Criterion 2: Desk lamp
"The lamp must give off a nice amount of light that helps students focus."
Criterion 3: Phone stand
"The stand must hold a phone at between 60° and 75° without tipping when placed on a flat surface and nudged with one finger."
Criterion 4: Reusable bag
"The bag must be eco-friendly, look modern, and be the kind of thing people want to be seen using."
Thumbs up = accept it   |   Thumbs sideways = needs work   |   Thumbs down = reject it
Debrief
Sort It: Answers
✅
Criterion 1: ACCEPT
Specific (99%), testable (before/after photos), achievable, and directly relevant. This is a strong criterion.
❌
Criterion 2: REJECT (Vague + Untestable)
Fix: "Must illuminate a 60cm × 60cm desk area to at least 300 lux, measured with a light meter at desk height."
✅
Criterion 3: ACCEPT
Precise angle range, a clear physical test (nudge), measurable outcome. Well written.
❌
Criterion 4: REJECT (Vague × 3)
"Eco-friendly," "modern," and "want to be seen using" are all unmeasurable opinions packed into one criterion. Split into separate, specific criteria.
Fix It  →  Rewrite
We Do
On your whiteboard, rewrite this criterion so it passes the 4-question test. Be ready to share.
❌ Criterion to Fix: Reusable Coffee Cup
"The cup must feel comfortable in the hand and be the right temperature on the outside so it doesn't burn people."
Scaffold: think about
  1. What does "comfortable in the hand" actually mean? Can you measure grip circumference or weight?
  2. What temperature range is safe to hold? (Hint: skin discomfort begins around 48°C)
  3. How long must it maintain a safe exterior temperature after filling?
  4. How would you test this?
Chin your boards when you're done, then we'll cold-call two or three rewrites to compare.
Reference
Categories for Design Criteria
Use these categories to help you think of criteria that cover different aspects of your design. You don't need to use all of them, but aim to cover more than one.
⚙️ How it Works
Performance, speed, reliability, compatibility. Does it do what it's supposed to, consistently?
👤 Ease of Use
Time to learn, number of steps, accessibility. Can the right people use it without help?
📐 Physical
Size, weight, durability, materials, cost. What are the real-world constraints your design must meet?
🎨 Aesthetic
Appearance, feel, emotional response. How should users feel when they see or use it?
🌱 Sustainability
Recycled materials, energy use, end-of-life disposal. What is the environmental impact?
⏳ Longevity
Lifespan, repairability, lasting relevance. Will it still be useful and functional over time?
Remember: each criterion must be specific, testable, achievable, and relevant to your users.
