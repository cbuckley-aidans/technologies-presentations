User Stories
Who are we designing for, and what do they actually need?
What is a user story?
Definition
A user story is a short statement that describes who your user is, what they want to do, and why it matters to them. It keeps your design focused on real people with real needs.
Why bother? It's easy to design for yourself. User stories force you to step into someone else's shoes and ask: what problem am I actually solving, and for whom?
Key Idea
User stories describe a goal, not a feature. You're not saying "the app needs a button." You're saying "the user needs to be able to do X quickly so they can Y."
I Do: The Format
As a... I want to... so that...
As a...
Who?
A specific type of user. Not just "a person" — be precise. A Year 8 student who plays sport. A teacher who moves between rooms.
→
I want to...
What?
The action or goal. Focus on what they're trying to achieve, not on how your design will work.
→
So that...
Why?
The benefit or outcome. This is the most important part. It explains why the goal matters.
Tip: if you can't fill in the "so that" part, your story probably isn't specific enough yet.
Worked Example
From vague to clear
I Do
Project: design a school locker organiser.
❌ Too Vague
"As a student, I want a better locker so that my stuff is organised."
1
"A student" — which student? What makes them specific?
2
"A better locker" is a solution, not a goal.
3
"Organised" tells us nothing about the real problem.
✓ Clear and Specific
"As a student who shares a locker, I want to quickly find my own books without disturbing my locker partner's things so that I'm not late to class."
✓ Another Angle — Same Project
"As a student who plays sport after school, I want to keep my gym kit separate from my school books so that my books don't get damp or smelly."
Same project. Different users, different needs.

❌ User Story to Fix: Homework Planner App
"As a user, I want the app to show my homework so I don't forget it."
Scaffold: think about
  1. Who specifically is this user? What makes their situation distinct?
  2. What do they actually need to do (not what the app does for them)?
  3. What goes wrong in their life when this need isn't met?
Cold call: two or three rewrites to compare. What changed, and why does it matter?
Reference
Types of Users to Consider
Good designs account for a range of users. Aim for 3–5 stories that cover different perspectives.
Primary Users
The main person using the design. Be specific: age, context, what they're doing when they use it.
Edge Case Users
Users with different needs: left-handed, vision impairment, different physical ability, or unusual context.
Secondary Users
People who interact with the design indirectly: a parent checking a planner, a teacher collecting work, a sibling sharing a product.
Different Contexts
Same user, different situation: rushed, tired, outdoors, distracted. How does context change what they need?
Emotional State
A stressed user needs different things than a relaxed one. What feeling should your design create or reduce?
First-time vs. Returning
New users need clarity; experienced users need efficiency. A good design serves both.
Remember: each story should describe a real goal, not a feature. Focus on what the user needs to achieve and why it matters to them.
