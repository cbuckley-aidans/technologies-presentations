“How Might We?”
Turning the problems you found into design opportunities.
What is a HMW?
Definition
A “How Might We” statement takes a problem you have observed and turns it into an open design opportunity: a question your app could answer.
Why not just say “the problem is...”? Problems close down thinking. HMW questions open it up: they invite creative solutions instead of complaints.
Compare
Problem statement
“Teenagers don’t know what’s in their food.”
HMW question
“How might we make nutrition info feel interesting so that teens actually want to read it?”
The Formula
“How might we  [ACTION]  so that  [OUTCOME]?”
⬤ ACTION — what your app does differently
⬤ OUTCOME — the change you want to see in people
Example
“How might we make building a balanced meal feel like a game so that students choose healthier options without being told to?”
⬤ ACTION
⬤ OUTCOME
Watch out: Too Broad
⚠ Too broad
“How might we fix healthy eating so that everyone is healthier?”
●
The action “fix healthy eating” could mean a thousand different things — there is no clear direction for your app.
●
The outcome “everyone is healthier” is unmeasurable — how would you ever know if your app worked?
●
A question this open leaves you paralysed at the design stage — you can’t sketch a feature from it.
Ask yourself
Could this question apply to a government policy, a school canteen, a doctor’s clinic, and an app all at once?
If yes → it’s too broad. Zoom in.
Narrow it by picking a specific user, moment, or behaviour. Who exactly? When exactly? What one thing?
Watch out: Too Narrow
ⓘ Too narrow
“How might we add a blue button on the home screen that shows calories so that users tap it?”
●
The action is a finished design decision, not an opportunity — you’ve already solved it before you started.
●
The outcome “users tap it” is a feature goal, not a real-world change for the user.
●
A question this specific shuts down creativity — there is only one possible answer.
Ask yourself
Does this question have only one possible answer? Does it describe a specific screen, button, or feature?
If yes → it’s too narrow. Step back to the why.
Zoom out by asking: what problem does that feature actually solve? What do we want the user to feel or do differently?
Just Right
✓ Good HMW
“How might we make it easy to compare the sugar content of drinks so that students can make a more informed choice when they’re thirsty?”
Specific enough
Focuses on one behaviour (comparing drinks), not all of healthy eating.
Open enough
Does not tell you how: you could solve it with a graph, a game, a badge, or a visual label.
Real outcome
The outcome is a human change (“make an informed choice”), not just a feature goal.
The Goldilocks test: specific enough to design from, open enough to have multiple creative solutions.

Fix these HMWs
We Do
Each statement below has a problem. On your whiteboard: identify what is wrong, then rewrite it as a better HMW.
1
“How might we make people eat salad so that nobody gets sick?”
Too Broad
Fix: "How might we make trying a new vegetable feel low-risk so that students are more willing to experiment with healthy foods?"
2
“How might we put a calorie counter in the top-right corner of the home screen so that users can see their total?”
Too Narrow
Fix: "How might we help students track what they eat throughout the day so that they notice patterns in their own habits?"
3
“How might we solve the global obesity crisis so that the world is a better place?”
Too Broad
Fix: "How might we make portion sizes easier to visualise so that students feel more in control of how much they eat?"
4
“How might we display a green tick on foods with less than 5g of sugar so that the tick appears?”
Too Narrow
Fix: "How might we make low-sugar options stand out at a glance so that students can make a quicker, more informed choice?"
