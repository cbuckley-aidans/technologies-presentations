Two people in the same house share a single router and internet connection. Here is what each of them needs to do right now.

Lily
Streaming a 4K movie
A very large, continuous download
Abby
Sending an email
A small, quick task

Both devices connect through the same router. The big question: what happens to Abby's email while Lily's movie is loading?

Step 03 of 06 Observe — What Did You Notice?
Think First

Did you see how Lily's massive file physically occupied the entire router pipe? How long was Abby forced to wait?

What happened in Simulation A
  • Lily's movie was sent as one large, unbroken file. It occupied the entire shared pipe.
  • While Lily's file was moving, Abby's email could not enter the router. The network was fully clogged.
  • Abby only got to send her email after Lily's entire movie had cleared the connection.

Step 05 of 06 Observe — What Changed?
Think First

Did Abby have to wait this time? How did chopping the data into blocks make the system fairer?

What happened in Simulation B
  • The router split both files into small, equal-sized packets and interleaved them through the connection.
  • Both Lily and Abby were sending at the same time. Neither one blocked the other entirely.
  • Because Abby's email only required a few packets, it finished very quickly while Lily's movie continued streaming.
Step 06 of 06 Summary — Why Packets Matter
Without Packets
  • Sent as one massive block
  • Monopolises the connection physically
  • Small tasks trapped waiting behind large ones
With Packets
  • Broken into manageable chunks
  • Router interleaves traffic from all users
  • Small tasks slip through quickly and fairly
