The Solution
The majority of Coober Pedy's infrastructure is built underground.
The earth surrounding a dugout absorbs heat during the day and releases it slowly overnight.
The underground temperature stays around 23–25°C year-round, regardless of what's happening on the surface.

Passive Cooling
Definition
Passive cooling is a sustainable architectural design approach that uses natural energy sources (wind, sun, and water) to lower indoor temperatures without mechanical air conditioning.
Example: Thermal Mass
One example of passive cooling is through the use of thermal mass. This uses materials like sandstone to absorb heat during the day and then release it slowly during the night.

The ancient Persians worked out a form of air conditioning over 700 years ago. Newer and more technological does not always mean better.
The Persian Windcatcher
Ancient Persian architects designed badgir (windcatchers) over 700 years ago, a form of natural air conditioning. Hot air always rises, so buildings were designed to capture prevailing wind at height and draw it down into the interior. These concepts are still applied in modern construction through the positioning of windows and insulation.

Six Evaluation Criteria
You are not expected to memorise these, but we will keep coming back to them. Selecting materials is often a balancing act across all six.
1. Thermal Mass
A material's ability to absorb, store, and slowly release heat.
2. Insulation
A material's resistance to heat transfer.
3. Embodied Carbon
The emissions produced in making and transporting a material.
4. Durability
How well a material resists wear, weather, and time.
5. Sustainability
Meeting current needs without compromising future ones.
6. Price
How affordably can this be mass produced?
Case Study: Hempcrete
What is it?
Hempcrete is a bio-composite building material made from hemp hurd (the woody inner fibres of the hemp plant), lime, and water. It is lightweight, breathable, and carbon-negative.

The Balancing Act
Every material will be a trade-off. Concrete is a great example of this tension.
Concrete: Strengths
Exceptionally strong, resisting heavy loads, rot, fire, and termites
Excellent thermal mass helps regulate indoor temperatures
Reducing heating and cooling costs over the building's life
↔
Concrete: Limitations
Cement production accounts for roughly 8% of global CO₂ emissions
Consumes approximately 10% of the world's industrial water
Contributes to urban heat island effects and habitat loss
This is why material scientists keep innovating, always trying to find materials that get closer to satisfying all six criteria.
Evaluating Innovation
We can use three questions to analyse how innovative a civil design is. These are not the only questions to ask, but they are quick and reliable to apply.
1
Does it perform better?
Stronger, faster, more resilient
2
Is it more sustainable?
Less waste, lower emissions, longer lifespan
3
Is it more accessible?
Cheaper, faster to build, usable in more places
Not all three need to be perfectly satisfied, and it is okay to find faults in an innovation. That's part of the analysis.

Case Study: Earthship
What is an Earthship?
Earthships are sustainable, off-grid, passive solar homes built from natural and repurposed materials such as earth-rammed tyres, bottles, and cans. Developed by architect Mike Reynolds, they are designed to be self-sufficient: generating their own power, harvesting water, treating sewage, and often growing food via integrated greenhouses.
However, consider Question 3: Ramming earth into tyres by hand is highly labour-intensive, making them slow and expensive to build. Mike Reynolds has acknowledged that prices are too high and continues to innovate to bring them down.
