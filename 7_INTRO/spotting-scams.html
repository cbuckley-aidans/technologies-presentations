Learning Intention
We are learning to identify specific indicators that distinguish phishing and scam attempts from legitimate communications.
Success Criteria
I can identify at least two specific indicators that a message is a phishing or scam attempt.
I can explain why each indicator is suspicious, not just state that it is.

The Red Flags
Read together
Scammers use predictable tricks. Knowing the patterns means you can spot them even when the message looks convincing.
The Sender
Misspelled domains, free email services (Gmail, Hotmail) pretending to be official, unknown numbers, or a known contact behaving strangely.
The Message
Urgency or threats, too-good-to-be-true offers, unexpected requests, requests for gift cards or wire transfers, fear tactics.
The Link or Action
Shortened URLs, links that don't match the claimed sender, requests for passwords or financial details, downloading unexpected files.
Modelled Example
Let me show you how I think
I Do
Scenario
You receive an email from refunds@ato-gov.com.au saying you are owed a $340 tax refund. It asks you to click a link and enter your bank account details to receive the money within 24 hours or the refund will expire.
I'm going to think out loud and name exactly what I notice...
Indicator 1 — Suspicious Sender Domain
The real ATO emails from @ato.gov.au. This email uses ato-gov.com.au — a fake domain designed to look official. The hyphen and the .com instead of .gov are the giveaway.
Indicator 2 — Artificial Urgency + Sensitive Info Request
The 24-hour deadline is a pressure tactic to stop you thinking clearly. Legitimate government agencies never ask for your bank details via email link — they use MyGov or direct deposit they already have on file.
Key habit: Always check the actual email address, not just the display name. Scammers often set the display name to "ATO" or "PayPal" while the real address is completely different.
The rule: Legitimate organisations will never ask for passwords, PINs, or full bank details through a link in an email or SMS.

Scenario
You get a text from an unknown number: "Hi! Australia Post: Your parcel could not be delivered. A small redelivery fee of $3.50 is required. Pay here: auspost-rebook.com/track"
On your whiteboard — write two specific indicators this is a scam.
60 seconds. Then chin your boards.
Indicator 1
Unknown sender number. Australia Post sends SMS from a registered short code or verified sender ID — not a random mobile number. Any parcel notification from an unknown number is immediately suspicious.
Indicator 2
Fake domain in the link. The real Australia Post site is auspost.com.au. The link auspost-rebook.com is a lookalike domain — different extension and a hyphen added to seem legitimate.

Scenario
You receive a WhatsApp message from an unknown number: "Hi [your name], it's Mum! I dropped my phone in the pool and this is my new number. I need you to buy two $100 Google Play gift cards urgently and send me the codes — I'll pay you back tonight."
Jot your two indicators, then turn and talk with your partner.
Indicator 1
Unknown number claiming to be a trusted person. This is the "Hi Mum / Hi Dad" scam. The claim that they "got a new phone" is a standard script to explain why you don't recognise the number. Always verify through a different channel before acting.
Indicator 2
Gift cards as payment. This is one of the clearest scam signals there is. No real person or organisation needs you to buy gift cards and read out the codes. Gift cards are untraceable — scammers love them for exactly that reason.

Scenario
A pop-up appears in your browser while watching a video: "ALERT: Your computer is infected with 3 viruses! Your personal files are at risk. Call Microsoft Support immediately: 1800 555 234. Do not close this window." The pop-up plays a loud alarm sound.
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Browser pop-ups cannot detect viruses. A webpage has no ability to scan your device. Any website claiming to have found a virus on your computer is lying — this is a fear tactic designed to make you panic and act fast.
Indicator 2
Microsoft never contacts you unsolicited. Legitimate tech companies do not phone you about problems — you contact them. A phone number in a browser pop-up is always a scammer waiting to ask for remote access to your device.

Scenario
You receive an Instagram DM from an account called @NikePromo.Official2024 with 3 posts and 40 followers: "Congratulations! You've been selected for our brand ambassador program. We're sending you FREE Nike gear — we just need your address and a $9.99 shipping fee paid via PayPal."
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Fake brand account. Nike's real Instagram is @nike — verified, millions of followers. An account with 3 posts, 40 followers, and numbers in the name is impersonating the brand. Scammers create these accounts quickly and discard them.
Indicator 2
Paying for something "free." Requesting payment for shipping on a "free" prize is a classic scam. Once you pay, the item never arrives — and they now have your payment details and home address.

Scenario
You receive an email with the subject "URGENT — Your Netflix account will be cancelled in 24 hours." The email is from billing@netflix-accounts-support.com and asks you to click a link to update your payment method or lose your subscription immediately.
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Fake sender domain. Netflix emails only come from @netflix.com. The domain netflix-accounts-support.com is a lookalike — extra words and hyphens are added to seem credible. The real domain is always short and clean.
Indicator 2
Artificial 24-hour urgency. The countdown is designed to make you act before you think. Real subscription services send multiple warning emails over weeks — they don't cancel your account after one email with a same-day deadline.

Scenario
You see a Facebook ad: "Woolworths is giving away $500 gift cards to celebrate 100 years! Only 50 left. Click here, answer 3 quick questions, and share with 10 friends to claim yours." The ad links to woolworths-giveaway2024.net
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Lookalike website domain. Woolworths' real site is woolworths.com.au. The link woolworths-giveaway2024.net uses a different extension (.net vs .com.au) and adds extra words — a clear sign of a fake site.
Indicator 2
Share to claim. Requiring you to share with friends is not generosity — it's a way to spread the scam further using your trusted name. Real competitions by major brands do not require sharing as a condition of entry.

You Do
Scenario
You receive a text message: "CBA BANK: Unusual sign-in detected on your account. Your access has been temporarily suspended. Verify now to restore access: bit.ly/cba-verif"
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Shortened URL hiding the real destination. bit.ly links conceal where they actually take you. Legitimate banks always link directly to their own domain (e.g. netbank.commbank.com.au) — they never use URL shorteners.
Indicator 2
Banks do not send SMS with clickable verification links. CommBank and other Australian banks explicitly state they will never ask you to verify your account via a link in a text message. If concerned, open the banking app directly or call the number on the back of your card.

Scenario
You receive a LinkedIn message from someone you don't know: "Hi! I came across your profile and think you'd be a perfect fit for our remote role — $120/hr, fully flexible. No experience needed. We'd love to get started straight away. Just send me your full name, date of birth, and bank account details so we can set up payroll."
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Too good to be true offer. $120/hr with no experience required, no interview, and an immediate start is not how real hiring works. Scammers use unrealistically attractive offers to bypass your critical thinking.
Indicator 2
Requesting sensitive personal data before employment is confirmed. No legitimate employer asks for your bank account details, date of birth, or full identification before you have signed a contract, completed onboarding, or even had an interview.
Scenario 9
You Do
Scenario
You receive a call from someone claiming to be from the Australian Federal Police: "This is an automated message from the AFP. A warrant has been issued for your arrest due to tax fraud. To stop this arrest you must purchase $2,000 in iTunes gift cards and call us back immediately on this number."
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Government agencies do not demand gift card payments. The AFP, ATO, and any legitimate government body will never instruct you to pay fines or avoid arrest via iTunes cards. This is an immediate and definitive scam signal — no exceptions.
Indicator 2
Arrest threats by automated call. Police do not issue arrest warrants by automated phone message. Real legal proceedings involve formal written notices. The threat of immediate arrest is a high-pressure fear tactic designed to override rational thinking.

Scenario
Your friend sends you a direct message on Discord: "dude you have to check this out I found a site where you can get free Robux, I already got 10,000. you just log in with your Roblox account here: roblox-free-robux.gg"
Two indicators — jot and chin.
Indicator 1
Free in-game currency is never legitimately available through third-party sites. Roblox only distributes Robux through its own platform. Any site claiming to give free Robux is a credential-harvesting site — it wants your login to take over your account.
Indicator 2
Your friend's account may already be compromised. Scam links spread by taking over accounts and sending messages to contact lists — so the message appearing to come from your friend is not verification it's safe. Your friend is likely already a victim.
