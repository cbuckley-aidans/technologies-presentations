Section 1 — What is Binary?
Humans — Base 10
We use the digits 0–9. Ten digits, ten fingers. This system is called Decimal.
Computers — Base 2
Computers only use two digits: 0 and 1. This system is called Binary. A single binary digit is called a bit.
Everything on a computer — images, videos, games, documents — is stored as a string of 0s and 1s.

One bit is the smallest possible piece of information a computer can store.
Section 2 — The Hardware
Meet the Transistor
What is a Transistor?
A transistor is a microscopic switch. Modern computer chips pack billions of them onto a chip smaller than your fingernail.
Two and only two states
Like any switch, a transistor can only ever be in one of two states at any moment:

ON  — electricity is flowing
OFF  — no electricity is flowing

Section 2 — How Circuits Work
The Classroom Switch Analogy
A transistor works exactly like the light switch on your classroom wall. It is either letting electricity flow (ON) or blocking it (OFF). There is no in-between.
A circuit can only ever be in one of two states — and those two states map perfectly onto binary 1 and 0.

Section 3 — The Conclusion
Why Binary wins
Detecting which state a signal is in requires the computer to make a decision. The fewer options there are, the harder it is to get it wrong.
Decimal (Base 10)
10 Targets
Each zone is narrow. A small wobble in voltage can make a 7 look like an 8. This is a read error.
→
Binary (Base 2)
2 Targets
Each zone is huge. The signal can wobble a lot and still be clearly read as 1 or 0. No errors.
Using Binary creates a massive safety margin between ON and OFF — this is what makes computers reliable enough to run billions of operations without mistakes.
Section 4 — Speed
The Need for Speed
Because the decision is so simple — ON or OFF — computers can make it at extraordinary speed.
Switching speed
0
billion times / second
Modern transistors switch ON and OFF billions of times every second.
Signal speed
≈c
speed of light
Electrical signals inside a computer travel at nearly the speed of light.
Why this matters
Because the computer doesn't have to ask "Is this a 7 or an 8?" — it only has to ask "ON or OFF?" — it can process data without double-checking. Speed and accuracy at once.
